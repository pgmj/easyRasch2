# Summary

Rasch measurement theory (RMT) is a family of psychometric models used to turn
ordinal questionnaire and test data, such as patient-reported outcome measures
or ability tests, into interval-level measurements. A Rasch analysis yields
such measurements only when the data satisfy explicit, testable requirements.
`easyRasch2` is an R package that streamlines this workflow. It wraps
established estimation engines behind a single, consistently named set of
functions covering the four core criteria summarised by
@christensen_psychometric_2021 — unidimensionality, local independence, ordered
response-category thresholds, and invariance across subgroups — together with
targeting, reliability, and person fit. Each diagnostic returns a
publication-ready `knitr::kable()` table or a `ggplot2` figure that can be
dropped directly into a Quarto or R Markdown report, making a complete analysis
reproducible from a single script.

# Statement of need

A standard Rasch analysis requires the analyst to assemble many separate
procedures — item-fit statistics, local dependence checks, dimensionality
tests, differential item functioning (DIF) analyses, reliability indices, and
targeting plots. In R, these are found in different packages, each with its own
data formats, argument conventions, and output objects. Less experienced users
face a steep learning curve, and the resulting analyses can be difficult to
reproduce.

A more substantive problem is how model fit statistics are interpreted. Most
software and published guidance evaluate statistics such as mean-square (MSQ)
item fit or Yen's $Q_3$ residuals [@yen_scaling_1984] against fixed
rule-of-thumb cutoffs (for example, item fit MSQ values of 0.7–1.3). Such
cutoffs are, however, known to depend on sample size, targeting, and the number
of items and response categories. A fixed rule can therefore flag well-fitting
items in one sample and miss genuine misfit in another [@muller_item_2020;
@johansson_detecting_2025; @christensen2017].

`easyRasch2` addresses both problems. It gives applied psychometricians a
single coherent interface to the whole workflow, and it replaces fixed
rule-of-thumb cutoffs with sample-specific ones obtained by parametric
bootstrap. A Rasch or partial credit model is fitted to the observed data, and
a large number of datasets of the same dimensions are then simulated from the
estimated parameters. Because each simulated dataset is generated by a model
that fits by construction, the distribution of a fit statistic across
simulations describes the values expected under model fit for precisely the
design at hand. Observed statistics are compared against this reference
distribution to yield calibrated cutoffs or *p*-values; the approach and its
performance for item fit are evaluated in @johansson_detecting_2025. The same idea is applied to conditional
infit MSQ, $Q_3$ residuals [@christensen2017], the largest eigenvalue from a
principal component analysis of standardized residuals [@chou_checking_2010],
the Martin-Löf test [@christensenMonteCarloApproach2007], and ordinal
confirmatory factor analysis (CFA) fit indices and per-item factor loadings.

The intended users are researchers and students developing or validating
rating scales and tests in health, education, and psychology, where transparent
and reproducible measurement is increasingly expected.

# State of the field

Applied Rasch analysis has long been dominated by commercial, Windows-only
standalone programs, most prominently Winsteps [@winsteps] and RUMM2030
[@rumm2030]. These programs established the fixed-cutoff conventions discussed
above, and their closed-source, GUI-driven designs make fully scripted,
reproducible analyses difficult. Providing an open-source alternative that
also incorporates methodological developments unavailable in those programs
was a primary motivation for this package.

Within R, excellent building blocks exist. `eRm` performs conditional maximum
likelihood (CML) estimation of the Rasch family [@mair_erm_2007];
`psychotools` also supplies model-fitting infrastructure with CML [@psychotools]; `mirt`
offers a wide range of marginal maximum likelihood multidimensional item response theory models
[@chalmers_mirt_2012]; `iarm` implements conditional item fit and
item-restscore tests [@iarm]; `psychotree` and `partykit` fit Rasch trees for
DIF [@strobl_rasch_2015; @partykit]; and `lavaan` fits the confirmatory factor
models used here as a dimensionality check [@rosseel_lavaan_2012]. More
broadly, `TAM` [@tam] and `sirt` [@sirt] provide extensive IRT toolkits that
include Rasch-family models, and `ShinyItemAnalysis` [@shinyitemanalysis]
offers interactive psychometric analysis oriented towards teaching. None of
these packages provides simulation-based critical values integrated across a
Rasch validation workflow.

`easyRasch2` deliberately does not reimplement estimation. Instead, it builds
on several of these packages and contributes two things they do not: (1) a consistent
diagnostic-and-reporting layer spanning the entire workflow, with uniform
inputs and publication-ready output; and (2) the simulation-based critical
values described above.

The package is the third iteration of a codebase developed openly since 2022.
It began as `RISEkbmRasch`, written to standardise Rasch analysis within the
author's then research group at RISE Research Institutes of Sweden, and was
renamed `easyRasch` in 2024 as external interest grew; neither predecessor was
released on CRAN. `easyRasch2`, begun in 2026, is a rewrite of this codebase
into a CRAN-compliant package with proper namespacing, a smaller dependency
footprint, and a unified estimation engine; the parametric-bootstrap
*p*-values, the multiplicity correction, and the multiple-imputation workflow
for conditional item fit described below are new in this version.

Beyond the consistent workflow, `easyRasch2` provides several methods that are
otherwise available only in development packages outside CRAN, or, to the
author's knowledge, not at all. Conditional item fit statistics require
complete response data — a practical weakness with real-world questionnaire
data, where some missingness is the norm. `easyRasch2` lifts this restriction
through a multiple-imputation workflow that computes conditional infit MSQ
across imputed datasets and pools the results. The Martin-Löf test of unidimensionality is provided for polytomous items with Monte Carlo p-values
[@christensenMonteCarloApproach2007], rather than the dichotomous-only form
implemented elsewhere. The DIF-tree functionality brings the partial-gamma
effect-size methods for dichotomous and polytomous items of
@henninger_partial_2025, previously distributed only through GitHub, into a packaged, documented form, and is, to the author's
knowledge, the only implementation of the accompanying tree-stability
assessment (via `stablelearner::stabletree()`) recommended in that work
[@philipp_measuring_2018;@henninger_partial_2025]. Conditional item characteristic curves follow
@buchardt_visualizing_2023, whose `RASCHplot` package is distributed only via
GitHub; `easyRasch2` reimplements them on its native CML/WLE engine and
extends the approach with an integrated DIF mode that overlays group-wise
observed scores and annotates each item's partial-gamma coefficient with
Benjamini–Hochberg-adjusted p-values. The non-parametric bootstrap
item-restscore test [@johansson_detecting_2025] is similarly not implemented
in any other CRAN R package known to the author.

# Software design

Three design choices shape the package. First, a single estimation engine: item parameters are obtained by CML via `psychotools`, and person locations by Warm's weighted likelihood estimation (WLE) [@warm_weighted_1989], recently shown to outperform maximum likelihood, conditional, and Bayesian person estimators in bias and measurement error, and recommended as the default estimator for Rasch models [@kreinerSpecificObjectivityMeasurement2025]. CML was
chosen because it estimates item parameters without assumptions about the
person distribution, consistent with the measurement philosophy of the Rasch
tradition, and because the conditional fit statistics used throughout the
package follow naturally from it. The `eRm` package, which also uses CML, is
included only for Andersen's likelihood-ratio test, delegating to its
canonical implementation rather than reimplementing it; `mirt` is included for
the plausible values behind one reliability index, where no CML equivalent
exists, and as an optional marginal-maximum-likelihood estimator.

Second, consistent output: every function takes a respondent-by-item data
frame and returns either a `kable` table or a `ggplot2` object, with a
`"dataframe"` option for downstream processing. Every table and figure caption
reports the estimation sample size and missing-data policy, making the output
self-documenting.

Third, the simulation machinery is factored into reusable cutoff functions
(for example, `RMitemInfitCutoff()` and `RMlocdepQ3Cutoff()`) whose output is
passed to the corresponding diagnostic. Because the parametric bootstrap is
the computational bottleneck, simulations are parallelised through `mirai`
[@mirai]. Bootstrap *p*-values are available across the simulation-based
diagnostics and are multiplicity-corrected by default, using family-wise error
control via the Westfall–Young step-down maxT procedure on
bootstrap-studentised statistics [@westfall_resampling_1993;
@ferreira_methods_2024]. False-discovery-rate alternatives or uncorrected
marginal *p*-values can be requested instead. Person fit uses
resampling-based reference distributions instead of unreliable asymptotic
nulls [@sinharay_2016; @muller_item_2020].

# Research impact statement

`easyRasch2` is distributed on the Comprehensive R Archive Network (CRAN)
under the GPL (>= 3) licence, with development hosted on GitHub
(<https://github.com/pgmj/easyRasch2>), a documentation website
(<https://pgmj.github.io/easyRasch2/>), an automated test suite (84% line
coverage, run on every commit via GitHub Actions), and a worked vignette built
around an included PHQ-9 depression-screener dataset drawn from the U.S.
National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey.

The package has an established user community predating this submission. It was presented at the PMhealth conference
in 2023 and was the topic of an invited pre-conference workshop at the 2024
Scandinavian Applied Measurement Conference. Its documentation and worked
examples have grown iteratively over four years in response to feedback from
users and measurement specialists. The statistical core of the package — the
use of simulation-based critical values for Rasch item fit — has been described
and evaluated in a peer-reviewed methodological article
[@johansson_detecting_2025], so the software gives applied researchers direct
access to a validated method that is otherwise laborious to implement.

A companion module, `easyRasch2jmv`, for the open-source statistical
platform jamovi [@jamovi] makes most analyses available through a
point-and-click graphical interface, broadening access beyond the R
community. It builds directly on the CRAN release, so the graphical and
scripted workflows share a single, tested implementation of the underlying
methods and produce identical results.

# AI usage disclosure

Generative AI (Anthropic's Claude) was used substantially in developing
`easyRasch2`. Much of the implementation was produced by porting and
refactoring the author's earlier `easyRasch` code with large-language-model
assistance. AI tools also assisted in drafting this manuscript. All
AI-generated code and text were reviewed and tested by the author and verified against (a) the package's automated test suite, (b) reference implementations in
`eRm`, `mirt`, `catR`, and `TAM`, and (c) the published statistical literature cited here.
